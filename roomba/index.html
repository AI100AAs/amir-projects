1. Quiet Check-In

The robot wakes, checks the room map, reads pet posture, and stays dormant if the pet is sleeping.

2. Optional Invite

When the pet is alert and comfortable, the robot enters view slowly and offers a game without blocking escape routes.

3. Adaptive Play

The robot varies motion, pauses, and distance based on engagement. It rewards curiosity, not forced interaction.

4. Safety Retreat

If the pet freezes, avoids, or comfort drops, the robot backs away and records a stress warning.

5. Owner Escalation

Unclear body language, low sensor confidence, or high autonomy triggers a review notification.

6. Session Report

The robot docks and summarizes enrichment, stress signals, privacy choices, and design tradeoffs.

7. Human Bonding

The owner receives suggestions for direct play so the robot complements, rather than replaces, relationship time.

8. Design Reflection

Students compare what the robot optimizes with what animal experts, owners, and pets might value.

Reliability and Autonomy

Behaviour classifiers can mistake fear for calm or excitement for consent. A robust design needs uncertainty thresholds, rest defaults, speed caps, and owner escalation.

Discussion: When should the robot stop deciding and ask a human?
policy drift body language

Animal Expertise

Design choices made only by AI engineers can miss species-specific enrichment needs. Animal behaviour experts should shape state rules and reward functions.

Discussion: Who should define a good play session?
expert values welfare

Owner Relationship

The robot can reduce loneliness while owners are away, but it may also make care feel outsourced. The app includes a bonding prompt in the final report.

Discussion: What should remain a human responsibility?
attachment care work

Malicious Use

A mobile robot can learn home layouts and daily routines. Strong access control and local-only map storage reduce stalking, theft, and tampering risks.

Discussion: Which data should never leave the home?
home maps remote access

Corporate Incentives

Pet behaviour data can reveal illness, travel, household stress, or family changes. That makes welfare analytics useful but commercially tempting.

Discussion: Should pet health predictions be monetized?
surveillance advertising

Economic Impacts

Pet sitters and daycare workers do specialized emotional labour. Automation may not affect many jobs, but it can still displace meaningful work.

Discussion: What human services should the robot support instead of replacing?
animal care labor

Government and Welfare

Aggregated signals could expose widespread pet loneliness or health issues. Policy benefits depend on consent, anonymization, and responsible interpretation.

Discussion: When is aggregate pet data a public good?
policy data governance

Possible AI Futures

If AI companions become compelling caretakers, students can ask whether pets are companions, dependents, design users, or beings whose agency should constrain automation.

Discussion: Could a machine become a pet, or only imitate one?
synthetic companions

Classroom Debate

Teams can argue for owner convenience, animal welfare, privacy-first design, labour protection, or banning autonomous play without supervision.

stakeholders tradeoffs

Physical Prototype Guide

  1. Cut a circular cardboard body and mark a clear front direction.
  2. Add a paper sensor ring with labels for camera, distance, sound, and bumper sensors.
  3. Attach a removable toy module: string lure, treat chute, or puzzle flap.
  4. Add a visible stop button and soft bumper to represent safety constraints.
  5. Use sticky notes for robot states and move them as the storyboard changes.
  6. Present one failure case and one design fix during final critique.

Prototype goal: make the invisible AI decisions visible enough for students to critique them.
